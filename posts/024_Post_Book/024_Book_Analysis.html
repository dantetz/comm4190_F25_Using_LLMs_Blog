Question

What is the strongest objection to the book’s thesis that modern ML success follows from mathematical structure rather than engineering brute force?

Here is the strongest serious objection, stated as cleanly and forcefully as possible—not a straw man.

The strongest objection: structure is retrospective, not causal

The core objection is that Why Machines Learn mistakes explanatory elegance for causal primacy. In short: the mathematical structure the book celebrates may describe why systems work after they succeed, but it may not be what actually made them succeed.

The claim under attack

The book argues that modern ML works because:

  • optimization aligns with underlying mathematical regularities,
  • geometry, symmetry, and smoothness guide learning,
  • models succeed because the problems themselves are mathematically learnable.

The objection

Modern ML may work primarily because of brute-force scale and engineering heuristics, with mathematical structure emerging as a byproduct rather than a driver.

Put bluntly:

We didn’t succeed because we understood the math; we understood the math because brute-force systems surprisingly worked.

Three pillars of the objection

1. Empirical precedence: scale worked before theory

Many breakthroughs arrived before convincing mathematical explanations:

  • Deep learning success (2012–2018) preceded solid theoretical accounts
  • Transformers outperformed alternatives before we understood why attention worked
  • Overparameterization violated classical statistical intuition yet still worked

If mathematical structure were the primary cause, we would expect:

  • theory → architecture → success

Instead, we got:

  • scale + hacks → success → post hoc theory

This suggests engineering stumbled into structure, rather than structure guiding engineering.

2. The Bitter Lesson problem

Rich Sutton’s Bitter Lesson directly challenges the book’s framing:

Methods that leverage scale and general computation outperform methods that rely on human insight and structure.

Under this view:

  • The math doesn’t cause success
  • The math merely rationalizes success after scale makes it visible

What looks like elegant geometry may simply be:

“What gradient descent converges to when you throw obscene amounts of compute at it.”

This turns the book’s thesis upside down.

3. Messy domains contradict the structure-first story

If ML success depended mainly on deep mathematical structure, we’d expect:

  • cleaner behavior in language and social data
  • principled generalization
  • stable, interpretable representations

Instead we see:

  • hallucinations
  • brittle reasoning
  • shortcut learning
  • massive sensitivity to prompt phrasing

These failures suggest models rely less on intrinsic structure and more on:

  • statistical interpolation
  • memorization softened by scale

Which looks much closer to brute-force approximation than elegant mathematical learning.

The philosophical core of the objection

At bottom, this is a philosophy of science critique:

  • The book risks committing retrospective rationalization
  • It treats successful outcomes as evidence of prior necessity
  • It may confuse discoverable structure with structuring cause

In other words:

Just because success can be written in beautiful math does not mean the math explains the success.

Where this objection really bites

The objection is strongest when it claims:

  • Mathematical structure is descriptive, not generative
  • It explains why optimization converges, not why we reached the regime where convergence mattered
  • Engineering brute force is what opened the door; math just describes the room afterward

Why this objection doesn’t fully kill the book (but limits it)

To be fair, the book survives if its thesis is downgraded:

  • From “ML succeeds because of structure”
  • To “ML succeeds once scale reveals structure”

That distinction matters enormously.

If scale is primary, then:

  • progress depends on compute and capital
  • not on insight or elegance
  • which has political, economic, and institutional implications the book underplays

The strongest objection is that Why Machines Learn confuses mathematical explanation with causal origin: modern ML may work not because structure guides learning, but because brute-force scale forces success, after which structure becomes visible and articulable.

Flip this objection and defend the book against it

Good—this is the right move. A serious defense has to concede what’s true in the objection and then show why it doesn’t reach the core of the book’s claim.

Below is the strongest possible defense of Why Machines Learn against the “brute force first, structure second” critique.

Step 1: Concede the surface point (without giving up the thesis)

Yes—historically, engineering and scale came first. Yes—theory often lagged behind practice. Yes—many breakthroughs were empirical accidents.

But none of that shows that brute force is the cause of success. It only shows that human discovery of structure lagged its exploitation.

This distinction matters.

A phenomenon can be causally structural even if humans discover that structure late.

Step 2: Scale does not create structure — it reveals it

The book’s key counter is this:

  • Gradient descent, backprop, attention, and overparameterization do not work on arbitrary problems

  • They only work because the data-generating processes of the world are:

    • smooth,
    • redundant,
    • compositional,
    • low-dimensional in practice.

If brute force alone were enough:

  • random labels would generalize (they don’t),
  • arbitrary datasets would learn (they don’t),
  • scaling would always help (it often plateaus or collapses).

Scale amplifies alignment with structure — it does not substitute for it.

Step 3: The “Bitter Lesson” actually supports the book

Sutton’s lesson is often misread.

Sutton argues that:

General methods outperform hand-crafted structure.

But Why Machines Learn replies:

  • Gradient descent is itself a structural prior
  • Inductive bias has not disappeared — it has migrated into optimization geometry

In other words:

  • We didn’t remove structure
  • We embedded it more abstractly

The math of high-dimensional optimization is the structure.

The book’s claim is not:

“Humans must hard-code insight”

It is:

“The world cooperates with simple, universal learning rules.”

That is exactly what Sutton’s examples demonstrate.

Step 4: “Post hoc math” still explains necessity, not coincidence

The accusation of retrospective rationalization fails because:

  • The mathematics explains why failure modes are systematic

  • It predicts:

    • double descent,
    • benign overfitting,
    • scaling laws,
    • mode connectivity,
    • feature reuse.

These are not arbitrary artifacts of engineering.

They recur across:

  • architectures,
  • datasets,
  • labs,
  • tasks.

That level of invariance is strong evidence of underlying structure, not just empirical luck.

Step 5: Overparameterization is not brute force — it is geometry

Classically, more parameters meant more overfitting.

Modern ML broke that rule not accidentally, but because:

  • large models reshape the loss landscape,
  • widen basins,
  • linearize dynamics,
  • make optimization behave more predictably.

These are geometric facts, not engineering tricks.

So when critics say:

“We just made the models huge”

The correct response is:

“We moved into a regime where the math becomes friendly.”

Step 6: Messy domains don’t refute structure — they demonstrate it

Language models are messy, yes.

But the fact that:

  • a single objective,
  • a single optimizer,
  • a single architecture class

can model:

  • syntax,
  • semantics,
  • translation,
  • coding,
  • reasoning at all

is extraordinary evidence that:

even human language has latent regularities exploitable by general mathematics.

The failures are real, but partial success at this scale is the stronger signal.

The book’s core defense, stated cleanly

Engineering scale did not replace mathematics; it exposed which mathematics mattered.

The causal story is not:

  • brute force → accidental success → invented theory

But:

  • world structure → scale accesses it → math describes it

One-sentence defense you can use verbatim

“The fact that theory lagged practice doesn’t mean structure is post hoc; it means we exploited real mathematical regularities before we understood them—scale reveals structure, it doesn’t create it.”
