Prompt

Im looking for a laptop to buy to practice excel modeling for my job (which will be on a desktop). Which laptop do you reccomend I buy to have the most similar keyboard layout to the on the job desktop.

Short answer: Buy a 15–16” Windows laptop with a full-size keyboard and numpad, and if you really want it to feel like work, also grab the same external desktop keyboard your office uses and plug it into the laptop.

Here’s how I’d think about it for Excel modeling:

1. What matters for “desktop-like” Excel practice

To make your laptop feel like the office desktop, you want:

  1. Windows, not Mac

    • Your finance job will almost certainly use Windows + Excel for Windows, which has some shortcuts that differ on Mac.

  2. Full-size keyboard with numpad

    • Separate numeric keypad on the right (for rapid number entry).
    • Full row of F1–F12 keys (for shortcuts like F2, F4, Alt+F8, etc.).
    • Dedicated Home / End / PgUp / PgDn / Insert / Delete if possible.

  3. Normal key sizes & spacing

    • Avoid ultra-compact 13–14” layouts that shrink keys or move things like the arrow cluster and Delete.

So you’re basically looking for a 15.6” or 16” Windows laptop with a numpad.

2. Concrete laptop recs (good → better → best)

All of these are Windows laptops with numpads, solid for Excel, and not crazy expensive:

A. Best “finance-vibes” pick: Lenovo ThinkPad E16 (Gen 1 or 2)

  • Why: ThinkPads are super common in banking/consulting; great keyboards, 16” screen, full numpad.
  • Example: Lenovo ThinkPad E16 Gen 1, 16” – 13th-gen Intel i5, 16 GB RAM, 512 GB SSD.
  • Rough price range: often $700–$850 on sale.
  • Feels very similar to a corporate ThinkPad you might actually get issued one day.

B. Solid, cheaper all-rounders: HP 15.6” laptops with numpad

  • HP’s 15.6” line typically has a full-size keyboard and numpad and is easy to find at big box stores.

  • Examples:

    • HP 15.6” Intel Core i5 / i7 models with 8–16 GB RAM, 512 GB SSD.

  • These are great if you want something simple and affordable but good enough for heavy Excel work.

C. Budget option: Dell Inspiron 15

  • Dell Inspiron 15 has a 15” display, enlarged keyboard with numpad, and is very budget-friendly.
  • Good if you just need cheap hardware to hammer shortcuts on, and your “real” machine will be the office desktop.

3. Power / specs (what’s “enough” for Excel)

For Excel modeling practice (even with some big models), aim for:

  • CPU: Recent Intel Core i5 / Ryzen 5 or better
  • RAM: 16 GB if you can afford it (8 GB is usable, but 16 is much nicer)
  • Storage: 256–512 GB SSD (any modern SSD is fine)

You don’t need a gaming GPU or anything fancy.

4. The move that makes it identical to work

If your office uses a separate desktop keyboard, the single best move is:

Buy the exact same keyboard model they use at work and plug it into your laptop via USB.

Then at home, you’re literally practicing on the same layout, same key feel, same travel as on the job. Any of the laptops above will then just be “a screen and a box” powering that keyboard.

TL;DR recommendation

If you want one clear pick:

Get a Lenovo ThinkPad E16 (Gen 1 or Gen 2) with 16 GB RAM and a numpad. It’ll feel very similar to a typical finance/IB/consulting work laptop, and Excel shortcuts will transfer 1:1.

If you tell me roughly budget (e.g. <$600, <$900, etc.) and whether you’ll travel with it a lot, I can narrow this to a single “buy this exact config” option.

My Recommendation

Given your goal — to practice Excel modelling and have keyboard layout similar to your job desktop — I’d lean toward the HP 15-fd0127dx, provided the price is acceptable.

Why? Because the HP explicitly confirms the numeric keypad and “full-size keyboard” which is a big deal when you’re doing a lot of number‐entry, modelling, spreadsheets. That numpad means you’ll use the same muscle memory as you will on your job desktop.

The Lenovo is a very good machine, but unless you verify that it does have the full numeric keypad and truly full-size keyboard, you might end up with a keyboard layout that’s a bit off from your desktop, which could cost you in productivity (and might slow you down or annoy you).

One Extra Drill-down I’d Do Before Buying

  • Check the exact keyboard layout photos for the Lenovo model you’re buying — zoom in on the listing, verify the numeric keypad (i.e., 0-9 keys on right side plus Enter, +-*, etc.).
  • Check for the key travel/feel if possible (reviews sometimes mention the feel of the keyboard) — for modelling you’ll want comfortable keys and a stable surface.
  • Confirm the port layout & upgrade possibilities (though less critical for Excel modelling if you mostly use it at home).
  • Consider if you might want to plug in an external monitor/keypad at home — if yes, then layout becomes slightly less critical but still helpful.
